Dart Coming Into Tara Station

360° View

Scene: First Dart Scene


Location: Loopline bridge, North Dock, Dublin

In the opening scenes of the movie there is plenty of b roll of the city. One of the more noticeable ones is the b roll of the view from the Dart (train) going south bound, towards Tara Dart Station, crossing over the River Liffey on the Loopline Bridge AKA Butt Bridge.


One major event in Irish history that included the Loopline Bridge was the 1916 Easter Rising. The British warship The Helga was called apon to stop the Irish rebellion. The Helga sailed up the River Liffey and stopped at the Loopline Bridge and shot over it and shelled Liberty Hall, the building in the bottom right of the shot, with her 12-pound guns.

Temple Bar

360° View

Scene: Drumming in the pawn shop


Location: Temple Bar, Dublin 2

The Band is shopping for a drum set from a pawn broker 'PJ Delaney' in Temple Bar. The band secure a drum set after Jimmy Rabbitte trades his mother's ornament in exchange.


The land on which Temple bar resides was a suburb outside the city walls in the 14th century. Temple Bar got its name from the Temple family of the early 1600s. The present-day Temple Bar is a centre for nightlife in Dublin. Temple Bar is also the location of some Dublins well know film and art establishments, The Irish Film Institute etc.

The Camden

360° View

Scene: Practice Scene


Location: 84 - 87 Camden Street Lower, Dublin 2

Without question, the prize for the best renovation has to go to the dilapidated pool hall above which the band rehearses. It was Ricardo’s Snooker Hall, built in 1912 as a movie palace, and given an art deco makeover in the Thirties. The old ‘rehearsal room’ was also seen in 1995’s An Awfully Big Adventure, with Hugh Grant and Alan Rickman.


It’s now the classy Camden Deluxe Hotel, 84-87 Camden Street Lower, south of Temple Bar. Thankfully the elaborately decorated arched ceiling has been preserved and now graces the Palace Nightclub, above the hotel proper. And to top it all, you can still play snooker here.

The Customs House

360° View

Scene: Lady of mann ship / going on holiday


Location: North Dock, Dublin 1

Imelda Quirke is about to go on her holidays with her boyfriend's family before changing her mind at the last second so she could be with the band for their next concert. In this shot you see the Lady of Mann docked on the Liffey. The prominent building in the background of the shot is the Customs House.


The Customs House was built by James Gandon, an English architect, in 1791. The Customs House was restored in 1921 after it was destroyed in a fire. The building's original use was to collect customs duties. But when Dublins port moved further down the building became obsolete. The Customs House was a part of major events in Irish history including the War of Independence. It now houses Local Government and Heritage.

The Mansion House

360° View

Scene: Jimmy lips meeting Wilson piquet


Location: Dawson St, Dublin 2

The Mansion House is where Joey 'The lips' convinced Jimmy Rabbitte to let him have a personal alone conversation with Wilson Pickett to try to get a favour from the famous American singer and songwriter on behalf of the band.


The Mansion house was built in 1710 by Joshua Dawson, the same Dawson the street was named after. The Round Room in the Mansion House is the location of the first Dáil assembly ("Assembly of Ireland") where Ireland declared its independence in 1919. The Mansion House is annually open to the public as part of Open House initiatives.

The Diving Bell

360° View

Scene: Jimmy Rabbit walking off and joey and jimmy last scene


Location: Dublin Docklands, Dublin

At the base of the Diving Bell Jimmy Rabbitte and Joey 'the lips' Fagan have their last scene together. The band's heated break-up after the concert leaves Jimmy angry with no hope. When Joey tries to shine a positive light on the situation, Jimmy is quick to call out Joey, on what he thought were lies. The pair goes their separate ways for the last time.


The Diving Bell was a device in which six men were placed under the bottomless bell that was compressed with air and lowered onto the bottom of the River Liffey to excavate the soil by hand. The Dublin Diving Bell was used from 1866 for 92 years to build and repair the walls of the quay during the development of Dublins port.
